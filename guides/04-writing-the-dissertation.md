# 04 — Writing the Dissertation

## Overview

This guide covers the structure, writing process, and style for your master's dissertation at IADE.

## Dissertation Structure

A typical master's dissertation includes:

### Front Matter
- **Title Page** — Title, your name, institution, date
- **Abstract** — 150–300 word summary of your entire work
- **Acknowledgments** — Thank your advisor and others
- **Table of Contents** — Auto-generated from your document

### Main Body
1. **Introduction** — Problem, objectives, research questions, structure overview
2. **Literature Review** — What's already known, gap in knowledge
3. **Methodology** — How you conducted your research
4. **Results** — What you found (raw presentation)
5. **Discussion** — What your results mean, comparison with literature
6. **Conclusion** — Summary, contributions, limitations, future work

### Back Matter
- **References** — Full citations in APA format
- **Appendices** — Supporting materials (surveys, raw data, code)

## Writing Process

### 1. Create an Outline

Before writing, map out each chapter with:
- Main points
- Supporting evidence
- Transitions between sections

### 2. Write the First Draft

Don't aim for perfection:
- Write **methodology** first (you know what you did)
- Then **results** (what you found)
- Then **literature review** and **introduction**
- **Abstract** and **conclusion** last

### 3. Revise and Edit

- **First pass** — Structure and flow
- **Second pass** — Clarity and arguments
- **Third pass** — Grammar, citations, formatting

## Academic Writing Style

### Do
- Use formal, objective language
- Write in third person (or first person if approved by advisor)
- Use past tense for methods and results
- Use present tense for established knowledge
- Be concise and direct

### Don't
- Use contractions (don't → do not)
- Use slang or informal expressions
- Use first person excessively
- Overuse passive voice
- Use vague language ("a lot", "very", "really")

### Writing Guidelines

- Write for your audience — assume readers may be unfamiliar with the topic
- Say as much as possible in as few words as necessary
- Avoid long sentences; use short paragraphs frequently
- Avoid superlatives in academic writing
- Write without spelling or grammatical errors
- Use punctuation correctly (avoid ellipsis and exclamation marks)
- Follow subject-verb agreement rules
- Link sentences, paragraphs, and chapters logically and coherently
- Respect your subject matter and the sources consulted
- Avoid clichés and common expressions
- Present original ideas
- Avoid ambiguous terms; define technical terms and concepts upon first use
- Demonstrate coherent, critical, and fluent reasoning throughout

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Writing introduction first | Write it last after you know your results |
| Mixing results and discussion | Keep them in separate chapters |
| Inconsistent tense | Past for methods/results, present for established facts |
| Missing transitions | Add linking sentences between sections |
| Plagiarism (intentional or not) | Always cite your sources properly |
